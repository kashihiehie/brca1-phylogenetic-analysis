The phylogenetic tree of BRCA1 shows an evolutionary relationship among all of the 10 species.In the tree it is observed that 
the mouse and rat clusters together tightly with a short branch length which shows their recent common ancestry with mammals.
Primates forms the other closely related pair which shows that they have been evolved recently.
The chicken shows larger branch length which shows greater sequence divergence from mammels. The invertebrates are more distantly
related, with branch lengths >0.25, consistent with their evolutionary separation from vertebrates approximately 600+ million 
years ago.

The overall short branch length in the tree shows that the BRCA1 is highly conserved across all of the species analysed i.e., 
from invertebrates to mammals. This conservation shows that the BRCA1 perform a very important function that is, DNA repair 
and tumor supression which is essential for all animals. The evolution has maintained this gene's sequence because mutations
that alter the BRCA1 would compromise its essential functions and can be harmful as well. This explains why BRCA1 mutations 
in humans have severe consequences and hence increases the breast cancer risk.

These findings support the hypothesis that BRCA1 is under strong purifying selection with little tolerance for the sequence 
variation. The presence of recognizable BRCA1 sequence in invertebrates like roundworm and drosophila indicates that the DNA 
repair mechanism was evolved veru early in animal evolution. This evolutionary conservation of BRCA1 brings out its fundamental 
importance in maintaining genomic stability across all animal life.
